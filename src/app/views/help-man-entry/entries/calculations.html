It depends on the calc method you choose in the preferences screen.

Widmark

This is the older and more widely recognized method. Basically, this is the amount of pure alcohol in your drink divided across the percentage of your body that will contain alcohol when you are intoxicated. Each drink increases your bac by (vol*abv)/(weight*r) where "r" is the "Widmark constant". The "Widmark constant" is the (estimated) fraction of the body mass in which alcohol would be present if it were distributed at concentrations equal to that in blood. Based on empirical studies, it averages 0.68 for men and 0.55 for women.

Watson

This is a newer, potentially more precise method. Each drink increases your bac by (vol*abv)(0.80 / TBW) where 0.80 is the percentage water in blood and TBW is your "Total Body Water". For men, TBW = 2.447-(0.09516*age)+(0.1074*height)+(0.3362*weight) For women, TBW = -2.097+(0.1069*height)+(0.2466*weight) Basically, this is the same as the Widmark method, but with a more complicated estimation of the equivalent of the "Widmark constant". No matter which calc method you choose, your BAC decreases by 0.015 per hour. The above explanations omit the unit conversions that need to be done.

You might notice that age/height only matter for the Watson method, and even then age only matters for men. If you want more information, look for an article called "Prediction of Blood Alcohol Concentration in Human Subjects" by Watson et. al. As of December 13th, 2010, the pdf can be found on Google Scholar
