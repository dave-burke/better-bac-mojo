About as accurate as a math equation can be when it simplifies an extremely complex biological process and generalizes it over very large populations. In all seriousness, the calculations used by this app are based on empirical studies, but every person is unique. There are a number of factors that could effect your BAC which are not accounted for by this app

Genetics

Your individual genetics play a significant role in how your body processes alcohol. Tiny mutations that vary by race can change how effectively your body breaks down alcohol1.

Food

Alcohol is absorbed more slowly in your stomach than in your small intestine. If you have a lot of food in your stomach, the alcohol you drink will take longer to get to your small intestine and will spend more time being absorbed slowly in your stomach1,2,3.

Medication

Many medications can severely inhibit your body's ability to break down alcohol leading to a higher peak BAC and a longer period of intoxication. "In one 1990 study, the average blood alcohol levels of those who took two maximum strength aspirin tablets before drinking were an average of 26 percent higher than those who were aspirin-free"1. "Women on birth control pills will process alcohol slower than other women, because the hormones in the pill and alcohol both rely on the liver for processing."2

Is your drink carbonated?

"If your drink is carbonated, the increased pressure in your stomach will force alcohol into your bloodstream faster."2

Elimination Rate

All other factors being equal, the average natural rate of elimination (used by this app) is 0.015 per hour. However, for some people this can be as low as 0.010 or as high as 0.018.3

Vomit

"Vomiting directly after consumption may prevent some alcohol from entering the blood stream, but waiting too long will do little to reduce BAC."2 Note that vomiting for the sole purpose of keeping your BAC low so that you can drink more would be a Bad Idea.

Tolerance

Frequent alcohol consumption makes your liver more efficient at processing it. This is called "Metabolic" tolerance, and it's different from "Functional" tolerance which diminishes the effects of alcohol, but not your BAC.3 Having a tolerance is probably not a good thing for your health.
